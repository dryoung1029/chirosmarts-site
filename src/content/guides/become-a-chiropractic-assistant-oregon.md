If you've just been hired at a chiropractic clinic in Oregon — or you're hoping to be — this guide covers the entire path to becoming a certified chiropractic assistant: what the role involves, exactly what the state requires, every step in order, what it costs, and how long it really takes. I've trained Oregon CAs for over a decade, and this is the walkthrough I wish every new hire received on day one.

## What a chiropractic assistant does — and why Oregon requires certification

A chiropractic assistant is far more than a front-desk role. Depending on the clinic, CAs take patient histories and vitals, set up and run therapies like electrical muscle stimulation, ultrasound, and hydrotherapy, assist with rehabilitation exercises, and keep the clinical side of the office running. Because some of those duties are hands-on patient care, Oregon regulates them: to perform clinical tasks, you must be certified by the Oregon Board of Chiropractic Examiners (OBCE). The certification exists to protect patients — and it protects you and your clinic too, because it documents that you were trained to do what you do.

Now each step, in the order you'll actually do them.

## Step 1: Get hired (most people start here)

You don't need certification to be *hired* — clinics routinely hire first and certify after, because the hands-on portion of training requires a supervising chiropractor anyway. What you can and can't do before certification is limited, so expect your clinic to want this process moving quickly. If you're job-hunting, telling an interviewer you already know the certification path (and have started the free portion of training) is a genuine edge.

## Step 2: Complete the 8 didactic hours

This is the structured instruction portion — anatomy, terminology, therapies, safety, scope, and the rules that govern what CAs may do. It can be completed online, at your own pace. Two practical tips. First, your training hours are a compliance record — make sure whoever provides your training tracks and can document them, because the board can audit. Second, spread the hours over several evenings rather than one marathon; you'll retain more, and the state exam at the end of this process is real.

## Step 3: Complete the 4 hands-on hours with your supervising chiropractor

## How long it takes

The training itself: as little as a week — two or three evenings for the 8 online hours, one scheduled session for the 4 hands-on hours. The full path to certification: usually several weeks end to end, dominated by application processing and exam scheduling rather than the training. The single best thing you can do for your timeline is submit a complete, correct application the first time.

## After you're certified: the part nobody tells you

Your certification renews **every year on the last day of your birth month**, with 6 hours of continuing education each year — including specific required topics (cultural competency every year; vitals training at your first renewal). Most lapses aren't laziness — they're CAs who simply never knew their deadline. Put it somewhere that reminds you.
